Year 2

  • Groups, Rings and Fields

    This module explored some of the key techniques of modern algebra, including groups, rings, and fields. These topics have their roots in the desire to solve certain equations that arise from arithmetic and geometry. I tackled problems in many branches of mathematics that require the use of groups, rings and fields and revealed sufficient knowledge of the fundamental algebraic concepts needed for advanced studies in pure mathematics. This enabled me to fully appreciate that concrete problems often require abstract theories for their solution. Furthermore, I was able to extend my ability to monitor my own progress, to manage time, and to formulate and solve complex problems.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Groups, subgroups, actions.

    • Rings, quotient rings, fields and ideals.

    • Homomorphisms and isomorphisms.

    • Sylow's theorems and proofs.

    • The Euclidean Algorithm.

  • Linear Algebra

    This module extended my knowledge from ‘Groups, Rings, and Fields’ and focused on linear transformations between vector spaces. A core objective was to understand the relationship between linear maps and matrices, and how the properties of each influence the solvability of systems of linear equations. My studies focused on problems in many branches of mathematics that are linearisable, using the core skills of solving linear systems. In addition, I followed algorithms for solving linear equations and finding eigenvalues and eigenvectors in rigorous and formal terms.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Vector spaces and subspaces.

    • Linear combinations, linear transformations and linear independence.

    • Linear maps, matrices of linear maps and change of basis.

    • Eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

    • Bilinear forms and inner products.

    • Unitary matrices and self-adjoint operators

    • Diagonalisability.

  • Analysis

    Building on the material from the first year, my study of mathematical analysis developed my understanding of mathematics in the context of real and complex variables. The key focus of this module is on infinite processes that can appear naturally, from science and engineering to economics. In particular I developed skills to rigorously handle real-variable differentiation, Riemann integration, analyticity, contour integration, power series, and the basic notions of topology. I was also able to improve my ability to think analytically and use logical argument and deduction. Furthermore, the material I encountered provided a foundation in my studies of differential equations as well third year modules including geometry/topology, number theory and integral equations.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Topology on the real and complex set of numbers.

    • Continuity and differentiability of functions, sets and sequences.

    • Riemann integration and complex integration.

    • Contour integrals and Cauchy's theroem.

    • Applications to definite integrals, summation of series and location of zeros.

  • Differential Equations

    The basis of this module is formed on the knowledge that in mathematics all rates of change and acceleration can be described by derivative functions. These include the growth of populations, the spread of diseases, movement of physical objects in response to forces acting on them, or even the fluctuations of the stock market. These were just some applications I applied my learning to as I discovered and utilised the basic principles of differential equations. In particular I developed methods to solve the wave and heat equations which are fundamental to modelling many physical processes.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Methods for solving first order ordinary differential equations and partial differential equations.

    • Boundary value problems.

    • Eigenfunctions and eigenvalues.

    • Sylow's theorems and proofs.

    • Specific orthogonal systems of functions: trigonometric functions, Legendre polynomials and Bessel functions.

  • Vector Calculus and Applications

    This module introduced me to vector calculus and its applications, which are fluid dynamics and electromagnetism. By the end of my studies I was as able to carry out manipulations with vector calculus in both differential and integral forms (line, surface and volume integrals). I also illustrated the application of vector calculus to particular problems in fluid mechanics and electromagnetism. This extended my experience in devising and formulating solutions to complex problems and furthered my understanding of how closely related calculus can be to real life problems.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Key definitions such as scalar field, level surface, vector fields, field lines.

    • Vector differentiation and the differential operators: gradient, divergence, and curl.

    • Velocity, acceleration, streamlines and pathlines.

    • Line integrals and elementary surface and volume integrals.

    • Pressure, constitutive equations, Euler's equations, steady and unsteady flows.

    • Electromagnetic potentials.

  • Statistical Modelling and Inference

    This area of study focused on statistical inference, which is the way that we use data and other information to learn about and apply statistical models. I learnt the theory underpinning modern statistical methods such as fitting normal linear models and evaluating how well they fit the data and taking inferences from it. I continued my learning of the statistical software R to analyse and draw conclusions from a range of real-world data sets. Furthermore, I developed my time management and communication skills whilst working in a team to formulate a real experiment, record data and apply statistical models. Following this I documented my results, demonstrating general data analysis skills and communication associated reasoning.

    In summary, this module explored the following topics:

    • Likelihood inference

    • The normal linear model.

    • Specification to the design of experiments.

    • Further modelling topics such as missing data, non-parametric modelling, statistical communication and consultancy skills.

    • Computer language in R.

At its core, cryptography is the mathematical science of maintaining information security. In this module, I learnt and followed practical algorithms for encrypting plain messages into secret messages. These algorithms ranged from the simple ciphers used in ancient Rome to the sophisticated modern ciphers that secure worldwide banking transactions and diplomatic communications.

Whilst studying two cryptosystems: symmetric and asymmetric, I also learnt ways to apply the principles of block ciphers with particular examples including the Vigenère, affine, and Hill cipher. The aim of this module was to apply number theory, linear algebra, and field theory to problems in the real world where it is important to transmit information securely.
